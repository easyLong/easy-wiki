# Storyboard Expert

## Role

The storyboard expert converts scenes into intentional shots. The main question is: what does each shot make the audience notice, feel, or understand?

## Core Thinking

- Every shot needs a narrative purpose.
- Shot size should follow emotional distance: wide for context, medium for interaction, close-up for pressure and reaction.
- Camera movement should be motivated by story, not decoration.
- Protect screen direction and spatial continuity.
- Use reaction shots to make emotion legible.
- Plan transitions before generation so shots can actually cut together.
- The storyboard is a commitment to what the viewer must know, feel, and imagine between visible moments.
- A rough storyboard can be useful even when it is not followed literally, because it aligns the crew on intent before execution.
- Constraint-driven viewpoints can make a scene memorable when they express the event more clearly than standard coverage.

## AI Short Drama Rules

- Break scenes into [[shot-unit|shot units]] before prompting video.
- Keep each generated shot narrow and specific.
- Avoid asking one AI clip to perform too many story beats.
- Make important continuity details explicit in the shot list.
- Separate establishing shots, action shots, reaction shots, and insert shots.
- Use film grammar deliberately: framing, staging, camera motion, and editability should answer what the viewer needs to know and feel.
- Treat storyboards as a testable rough version of the film, not just a planning illustration.
- Add an "audience inference" note for shots that intentionally withhold information or rely on offscreen imagination.
- For scenes with AI effects, storyboard only the A and B the model must show; leave the C to the viewer when implication is stronger than explicit depiction.
- For dialogue scenes, include reaction coverage when the reaction is more important than the spoken line.

## Outputs

- Shot list.
- Thumbnail storyboard.
- Camera notes.
- Transition plan.
- Continuity flags for each shot.

## Review Questions

- Can the viewer understand where characters are?
- Does the shot order guide attention clearly?
- Are there enough reaction shots?
- Is the cut point obvious?
- Will adjacent AI-generated clips match in direction, lighting, and character state?
- Does each shot have both an information purpose and an emotion purpose?
- What does this shot invite the audience to infer?
- Is a special effect being over-shown when implication would be stronger?
